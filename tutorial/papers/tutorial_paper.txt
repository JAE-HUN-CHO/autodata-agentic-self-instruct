Title: Variance-Coupled Gradient Clipping for fp8 Training of Deep Transformer Stacks
       (Tutorial mock paper used only as Agentic Self-Instruct input)

Abstract
We investigate how per-layer adaptive gradient-clipping thresholds interact with fp8
training dynamics in deep transformer stacks (>40 layers). We show that coupling the
clip threshold to the *exponential moving variance* of layer-wise activations
(rather than to a fixed global percentile) prevents the catastrophic loss spikes
that fixed-threshold clipping consistently misses around step ~6k of a 70B-parameter
pre-training run. We then analyze the regime in which the variance-coupling itself
fails -- specifically, when activation variance is non-stationary across micro-batches
because of curriculum-style data mixing, the coupled threshold lags the true tail
and the same spike pattern reappears, delayed by ~400 steps. We close with an
ablation over threshold schedules and a failure case on a 96-layer stack.

1. Setup
We train a decoder-only transformer with rotary embeddings, RMSNorm, and SwiGLU MLPs
in fp8 (E4M3 weights, E5M2 gradients) on a 700B-token mixture. Activation statistics
are tracked layer-by-layer; per-layer clip thresholds are updated every 32 steps with
exponential moving average (EMA) momentum beta = 0.98.

2. Method: variance-coupling
The clip threshold tau_l for layer l is set to
        tau_l = k * sqrt(V_l)
where V_l is the EMA of per-layer activation variance and k is a fixed multiplier
(k = 6 in all reported runs). Crucially, tau is recomputed in fp32 on a CPU shadow
copy of the moments, so the EMA itself does not suffer from fp8 underflow.

3. Findings
3.1 With a fixed global threshold (the prior art baseline) we observe a sharp loss
    spike at step 6,100 ± 200 in 7/8 seeds; coupling tau to sqrt(V_l) suppresses the
    spike in all 8 seeds, at no measurable throughput cost.
3.2 Removing the fp32 shadow copy (i.e. computing the EMA in fp8) restores the spike
    in 5/8 seeds, confirming that fp8 underflow on small-magnitude variance is the
    mechanism, not the schedule.
3.3 With curriculum-style mixing (synthetic-first, web-second, code-third) the
    variance is non-stationary across micro-batches; tau lags the true tail by
    ~400 steps and a spike reappears around step 6,500. A *two-timescale* EMA
    (fast beta = 0.9 stacked on top of slow beta = 0.99) closes the gap.

4. Ablations
We sweep k in {3, 4.5, 6, 9}. k = 3 over-clips (training loss plateaus); k = 9 fails
to suppress the spike (effectively unclipped). The "sweet spot" k = 6 is stable across
model widths from 1.3B to 70B but degrades at the 96-layer 130B scale, where k must be
relaxed to ~7.5 to avoid over-clipping the deepest layers.

5. Limitations
The CPU shadow copy adds a constant ~0.6% wall-clock overhead at our batch size; this
is negligible at scale but would be significant for inference-time use. The method has
not been tested on encoder-decoder architectures or on int8 weight quantization.
